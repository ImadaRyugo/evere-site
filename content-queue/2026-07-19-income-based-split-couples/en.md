For couples with an income gap, everything gets easier the moment you split expenses **by the same ratio instead of the same amount**. As a rule of thumb: if one take-home pay is 1.5× the other or more, that's the sign to rethink a 50/50 split.

This article walks through proportional splitting — how to calculate it and how to run it day to day — with concrete numbers.

## 🤔 50/50 is equal, but not always fair

Take a couple where A brings home $3,000 a month and B brings home $5,000. Say their shared spending (dates, groceries, trips) is $1,000 a month — split evenly, $500 each.

| | A | B |
|---|---|---|
| Take-home pay | $3,000 | $5,000 |
| Share (50/50) | $500 | $500 |
| As % of income | **16.7%** | 10% |

The same $500 costs A 16.7% of their income but B only 10%. Equal in dollars, nearly double in weight. Keep this up and the lower earner can't save, and starts hesitating before suggesting a date.

## 🧮 The proportional split, in 3 steps

**Step 1: Share your take-home numbers.** This is the biggest hurdle, but rough figures are enough — "about three grand and about five grand" works fine.

**Step 2: Compute the ratio.** Each person's share of the combined take-home:

> A: 3,000 ÷ (3,000 + 5,000) = 37.5% / B: 5,000 ÷ (3,000 + 5,000) = 62.5%

Don't bother being exact. Round to something clean like 40:60 — it's easier to run and easier to accept psychologically.

**Step 3: Apply the ratio to shared expenses.** On the $1,000 above: A pays $400, B pays $600. As a share of income that's about 13% and 12% — much closer to **"the same pinch."**

## 🗂️ What counts as a shared expense?

The ratio applies to shared spending only. A typical line:

- **Shared**: dates, meals together, trips, household items you both use
- **Personal**: each person's hobbies, individual shopping, nights out with your own friends

For the inevitable gray-zone purchases, don't debate each one — pick a default up front, either "when in doubt, it's shared" or "when in doubt, it's personal," and move on.

## ⚙️ Day to day: build a system where nobody does math

The weakness of proportional splitting is doing "so 64% of $68 is…" at every register. Two practical setups:

1. **Settle monthly** — record every fronted expense, apply the ratio to the monthly total, and settle just the difference once
2. **Use an app with a ratio mode** — in Evere, choose the "Ratio" [split mode](/en/docs/expenses/) when recording an expense, enter "60% / 40%," and each share is calculated automatically

Either way, one person pays the whole bill in the moment — no fiddly money passing mid-date, which is a nice side benefit.

## 💬 How to propose it to your partner

Proportional splitting is rational, but pitch it wrong and it can land as "so you're counting on my income?" The key is to lead with the goal — keeping the relationship on equal footing — not with a complaint about amounts:

> "Honestly, 50/50 has been tight for me, and I've caught myself wanting to skip dates over it. Could we split by ratio instead of amount? I think we'd both stress less about spending."

Frame it not as "you should pay more," but as "let's find the split that lets us enjoy the most as a team."

## 🔄 When to revisit the ratio

The ratio isn't set in stone. Revisit it when:

- Either of you changes jobs, gets a raise, or takes a pay cut
- You move in together (rent adds a big fixed cost)
- Income changes temporarily — parental leave, a career break, going back to school

Agreeing up front that "we'll revisit this when things change" makes it far easier to raise later.

## ❓ FAQ

**Q. How precise should the ratio be?**
50:50, 40:60, 30:70 — that level of granularity is plenty. A ratio you can both remember beats one that's accurate to the percentage point.

**Q. What if I don't want to disclose my income?**
If exact numbers feel like too much, just agree on the ratio directly: "the higher earner takes 60." What matters isn't precision — it's the two of you acknowledging the gap exists.

**Q. How does this interact with treating each other and gifts?**
The ratio is a rule for everyday shared expenses. Keep birthdays and anniversaries as exceptions — treating each other stays special precisely because the everyday rule exists.
