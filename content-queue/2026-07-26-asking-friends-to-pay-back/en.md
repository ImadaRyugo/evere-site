The rule for chasing money you fronted is: **early, light, and specific**. The longer you wait, the more "it's too late to bring it up now" takes hold; the smaller the amount, the more you dread looking cheap. Which means the very first ask, sent as soon as possible, is by far the easiest one.

This article covers the principles and gives you scripts you can send as-is.

## 😶 Why "you owe me" is so hard to say

Asking feels hard not because you're cheap. It's because two impulses are on a scale: "I don't want to damage a friendship over twenty bucks" and "but I do want my twenty bucks."

If you pick silence, the amount stays small but the memory — "that's the person who doesn't pay you back" — stays forever. Ask and get paid, and the friendship actually stays healthier. In most cases the other person simply forgot.

## 🎯 The three principles

1. **Early** — the same day or the next is the most natural window. "Last night came to $32 each" is an administrative note; the identical words a month later are a demand
2. **Light** — a serious tone makes people feel accused. Aim for the weight of a single emoji
3. **Specific** — "whenever" is a trap. Name the amount and a deadline (or how to send it) so they can act immediately

## ✉️ Scripts by situation

### Same day or next day (the easy one)

> Last night was fun! The bill worked out to $32 each — Venmo or Cash App whenever, whatever's easier 🙏

> Lunch came to $11.50! No rush at all, just sending it now before I forget (paper trail lol)

### A week later (past the easy window)

> Hey — just remembered the dinner last week ($32), I don't think that one ever got sent? Just double-checking!

> Reminder from your friendly neighborhood accountant: $32 from last week's dinner 😄 Sometime this week would be great!

The key is not to test their memory ("do you remember?") but to frame it as you being the one who just remembered.

### More than a month later

> Sorry this is so late — I think the $32 from the dinner back in March never made it over! Whenever you get a chance is totally fine

For a long-delayed ask, saying "sorry it's late" yourself is the fastest path. You don't actually owe an apology, but that phrase takes the edge off.

### To a whole group at once

> [Dinner settle-up] $32 each! If you could send it this week that'd be great 🙏 Drop a 👍 once you've sent so I can keep track

Better than individual messages: nobody feels singled out, and seeing who's already paid creates gentle, natural pressure on the rest.

### When someone repeatedly doesn't pay

> Sorry to keep bringing this up — could you get me the $32 from March by the end of the month? Happy to take it in installments if that helps, just let me know!

At this point, prioritize a clear deadline over lightness. If that still doesn't move them, treat it as information: this isn't someone to front money for again.

## 🙋 The classy way to respond when you're the one being asked

If you're on the receiving end, there's exactly one line: "Ugh, sorry — totally forgot! Sending now." Send first, explain never. That's the shortest path to keeping trust.

## ⚙️ The system that makes asking unnecessary

The best fix is needing these scripts less often.

One is to settle on the spot: pair the check with the transfer, right there at the table.

The other is to share the record. Log what you fronted in a bill-splitting app and share it, and "who owes whom what" stays permanently visible. The request stops coming from your mouth and becomes a fact displayed on a screen, which removes the awkwardness at its source.

Evere is built around this idea: record what you front, and the amounts organize themselves at settlement time. The biggest benefit of a shared record is that "you owe me" turns into "let's settle up."

## ❓ FAQ

**Q. Is there a minimum amount worth chasing?**
There's no floor. Five dollars fronted is still five dollars fronted. That said, for pocket change you can soften the collection — "get the next coffee and we're even" works fine.

**Q. What if it's my boss or someone senior to me?**
Keep the tone administrative: "Following up on the cost from the other day." For work dinners, route it through the organizer or a group-wide message so it never becomes a person-to-person ask.

**Q. I'm scared asking will damage the friendship.**
With a decent person, asking doesn't break anything. And if a friendship breaks over a $30 reminder, the cause wasn't the reminder — it was the looseness with money.
