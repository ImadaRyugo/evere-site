Money trouble at a group dinner almost never starts at the table when the check lands. **Nine times out of ten, the cause is something nobody decided in advance.** Which is good news: if you announce the cost, the drinks policy, the rounding rule, and how late arrivals are handled, your job as organizer is essentially done before anyone shows up.

This article breaks the organizer's job into three phases: before, during, and after.

## 📣 Before: the announcement decides 90% of it

### Give a cost estimate up front

Just saying "figure on $45–55 a head" in the invite kills the "that was more than I expected" complaint before it exists. For a set menu, give the exact number; for à la carte, give a ceiling.

### Decide how and when you're collecting

| Method | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| Cash on the night | Done and dusted on the spot | Making change is a pain; late arrivals slip away |
| Collect in advance | Protects you against no-shows | Moves the work earlier |
| Transfers on the night or after | No change needed; leaves a record | "I'll send it later" is where collection goes to die |

If you're collecting digitally, set a deadline: "please send it tonight." "Whenever's fine" is exactly how the organizer ends up fronting the money for three weeks.

### The tipping question

If tip is added to the bill or you're leaving one, decide before the meal whether it's split into the per-head cost. Announce "about $50 including tax and tip" rather than quoting a pre-tip number and surprising people. Splitting the tip with the bill is by far the simplest.

## 🏮 During: exceptions and edge cases

### People who aren't drinking

The most common flashpoint. A flat rule is easiest: "if you're not drinking, take $15 off." Decide this at invite time, not at the table — negotiating it in the moment, in front of everyone, is how you make somebody feel like a nuisance.

### Tiered amounts

If the group spans seniority or big income differences and someone wants to cover more, don't compute proportions — round it into two or three tiers: $70 / $55 / $40. Set the tiers so the total lands slightly above the bill, and use the surplus for rounding.

### Late arrivals and early leavers

- Late arrival: a time-based rule like "an hour or more, full share; less, half"
- Early leaver: full share for a set menu; for à la carte, estimate up to when they left

Precision matters less than having decided the rule beforehand.

## 🪙 Rounding: five options

What to do with the remainder ($42.38 a head, say):

1. **Round up, difference goes to the organizer** — round to the nearest dollar or five; the surplus is a nod to the work you put in
2. **Organizer eats the remainder** — collect a round number and absorb the shortfall. Fine for small, casual groups
3. **The heavy drinker or the person who suggested it covers it** — works if the group has that kind of personality
4. **Roll it into next time** — for groups that meet regularly, bank the surplus toward the next dinner
5. **Let the app handle it** — a bill-splitting app assigns the remainder automatically

Any of them is fine. The one rule you can't skip: be transparent about where the leftover money goes. "I have no idea where the extra went" is the doorway to distrust.

## 🌙 Treat the bar afterward as a separate event

Mix the dinner and the bar after into one bill and you'll charge people for something they didn't attend — guaranteed trouble. Keep the second venue completely separate in both attendees and accounting, and feel free to hand the organizer role to someone else.

## 📥 After: make collection a system, not a favor

If you put the whole thing on your card, collection can drag on for days. This is where shared records earn their keep.

- Share a list of attendees, amounts, and payment status. Just making "who hasn't paid" visible removes almost all need to chase
- Record the dinner in a bill-splitting app and invite the attendees, and "who owes the organizer what" is visible to everyone

Sending individual "hey, still waiting" messages is a real emotional tax on the organizer. The trick is to separate the request from your personality and let the system do the asking.

## ❓ FAQ

**Q. Is it unfair that the organizer racks up card points fronting the whole bill?**
Weighed against booking, collecting, and chasing, points are generally considered fair compensation. If it bothers the group, rotate who fronts.

**Q. Can I charge someone who cancels last minute?**
If the restaurant charged you a cancellation fee, billing the actual cost is entirely reasonable. Adding "cancellations after the day before may incur a fee" to the invite makes that ask dramatically easier.

**Q. The amount I'd be fronting makes me nervous.**
For an expensive event, switch to collecting in advance. Alternatively, pick a venue that lets people pay their share on the spot, which shrinks what you front in the first place.
