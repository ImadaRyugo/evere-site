Tipping causes arguments out of proportion to the amounts involved, because **a tip isn't one thing**. There's the charge already on the bill, the money you leave with a meal, and what you hand someone for a service you personally received. Treat all three the same and no line can be drawn.

The test is simple: **did everyone receive that service?** That question settles almost every case.

## Tips come in three kinds

| Kind | Example | Goes in the split? |
|---|---|---|
| Service charge on the bill | Already added to the check | Yes (part of the face amount) |
| Tip left with a meal | Cash on the table, or added on the terminal | Yes |
| Tip for a service you personally received | Porter, housekeeping, a private guide | No (on you) |

The first two pay for something everyone received, so they're part of that expense. The third is decided by the person who received it, and paid by that person. Whether you have your bag carried is your call, so there's no reason to spread it.

## The service charge needs no thought

When the check already includes a service charge, that's simply part of the face amount. Record the total including it and you're done.

Propose "let's split the pre-service-charge amount" and you've invented a receipt-recalculation task. Everyone had the same meal; there's no reason to carve that piece out.

## A meal's tip is part of the meal

Cash on the table, or a tip added on the card terminal, is payment for that meal. Record the meal and the tip as one expense and the split comes out right on its own.

Trouble starts when someone else covers just the tip.

> "I'll put the bill on my card."
> "Cool, I'll leave the tip in cash."

That's the moment the tip disappears from the record. The card payment leaves a receipt; the cash on the table leaves nothing. Wave it off as small change and it repeats all week.

There's one fix: whoever puts down the tip records one line, right there. Small amounts still get folded into the settlement automatically, as long as the record exists. Why cash disappears from records is covered in [tracking who paid abroad](/en/blog/mixed-payment-methods-abroad/).

## Personal tips stay out of the split

Housekeeping, the porter who took your bags, the transfer you alone booked. Whoever received it pays. That's the end of it.

The line isn't there out of stinginess. Once discretionary spending enters the split, the group has to agree on whether to tip at all, and how much. Someone who wants to be generous would be spending other people's money. That doesn't feel good to anyone.

Same logic covers exchange fees and card foreign transaction fees: personal ([how to split bills abroad](/en/blog/split-bills-different-currencies/)).

## Agree on one sentence before you go

Heading somewhere with a tipping culture? Agree on this much and you're covered.

> Tips on meals go in with the meal and get split. Personal tips are your own.

That one line resolves every decision you'll face on the ground. A single sentence everyone remembers beats a detailed rulebook nobody does.

## ❓ FAQ

**Q. The group disagrees about how much to tip.**
The amount is decided by whoever is at the table paying. Second-guessing it afterward isn't worth it. If it really bothers you, pick up the next meal yourself.

**Q. What if someone in the group comes from a country without tipping?**
Share up front that you'll follow local custom. When someone declines to tip because they didn't know, it's the people sitting with them who feel it.

**Q. What about terminals that ask "what percentage?"**
Whatever you pick goes onto the bill, so record the total as-is. It's payment for a meal everyone had, so it's part of the split.
