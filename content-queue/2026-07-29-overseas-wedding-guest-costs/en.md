Money gets ugly around destination weddings because the couple and their guests arrive with **different assumptions about what counts as self-funded**. At a wedding down the road, common sense fills that gap. Fly everyone abroad and the gap becomes hundreds or thousands of dollars, out in the open.

Here's what to settle, from both sides.

## ✈️ A guest's costs come in three layers

What a destination-wedding guest spends splits into three very different things.

| Layer | What it covers | Who usually pays |
|---|---|---|
| Getting there and staying | Flights, hotel, local transport | The guest |
| The wedding itself | Venue, ceremony, the meal | The couple (they invited you) |
| Everything around it | After-party, sightseeing, rental car, outfits | Split among whoever joins |

Layers one and three are where it goes wrong. **The couple covers the wedding, but getting to the wedding is on the guest** — and that's the piece that differs completely from a wedding at home.

## 💰 The gift question

When guests are already paying for flights and a hotel, a scaled-back gift — or none at all — is a reasonable expectation. Plenty of couples say outright that gifts aren't expected.

What matters isn't the amount. It's that the couple says it first. No guest wants to ask "so… are we doing gifts?" And if nobody asks, everyone decides privately, and then the people who brought something stand next to the people who didn't. That awkwardness lands squarely on your guests.

## 📣 Four things the couple should say up front

Put these in the invitation itself.

1. **What guests are expected to cover** (flights and hotel are on you, etc.)
2. **A rough per-person total** ("budget around $1,500 including airfare")
3. **Where you stand on gifts**
4. **That saying no changes nothing between you**

Don't skip the fourth. With a destination wedding, the hardest part is how difficult it is to decline. Handing someone a graceful way out, when the price tag is the reason they can't come, is the host's job.

## 🧾 Splitting what guests spend together

Guests always end up with shared costs around the wedding. The taxi from the airport, a rental car, the after-party, group photos, a joint gift for the couple.

Keep all of that separate from the couple's budget. Mix them and nobody can tell who carried what. Set up one group among the guests and have whoever pays record it right there.

Currencies get mixed in too, so record face amounts in the local currency. The approach is the same as [how to split bills abroad](/en/blog/split-bills-different-currencies/): settle once, after everyone's home.

## 🙅 On saying you can't come

A word for the guests. Sometimes you can't go, because of the money. That isn't rude and it isn't cold.

Say it early. The couple wants the headcount locked while flights are still cheap, so every day you spend agonizing costs you both. "That window is just impossible for us — let us celebrate with you here instead" does the job.

Get the money conversation out of the way first, and the day itself gets to be about the wedding. That's the part of destination-wedding planning that actually pays off.
