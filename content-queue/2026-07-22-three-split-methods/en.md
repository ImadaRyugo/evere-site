Every bill split in the world, no matter how creative, reduces to three methods: **equal (divide by headcount), ratio (allocate by proportion), and manual (assign exact amounts)**. Master when to use which and you'll almost never again wonder "what's the fair way to split this?" — at dinners, on trips, or at home.

The big picture first:

| Method | How it splits | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| Equal | Amount ÷ people | Group dinners, anything shared by everyone |
| Ratio | Allocated by an agreed proportion | Income-gap couples, usage-gap costs |
| Manual | Exact amount per person | Itemized orders, costs that only apply to some |

## ➗ Method 1: Equal — the default

Divide the total by the number of people. The most basic method.

**Best for expenses where individual usage is hard or pointless to distinguish**: a set-menu dinner, a shared taxi, the condiments everyone used.

Equal's only weakness is remainders. $100 across 3 people is $33.33… — someone has to pay a cent more. Either pre-agree a rule ("organizer absorbs it," "whoever fronted the most pays slightly less") or use an app that assigns remainders automatically.

## ⚖️ Method 2: Ratio — when equal amounts aren't equal at all

Allocate by an agreed proportion (40:60, 1:2:2, and so on).

**Best when there's a clear, recognized gap in ability to pay or in usage**:

- A couple with an income gap splitting shared expenses (say 40:60 by take-home pay)
- Utilities between someone working from home and someone at the office
- Roommate rent when rooms differ in size
- A trip with two adults and one child (split 2:2:1)

Splitting $2,000 rent at "bigger room : smaller room = 6:4" gives $1,200 and $800. The strength of this method: agree on the ratio once, and it applies mechanically to any amount thereafter.

## ✍️ Method 3: Manual — when "everyone pays their own" is clear-cut

Assign each person's exact amount directly.

**Best when consumption is clearly separated**:

- An itemized meal where everyone ordered their own things
- A bulk purchase where some items belong to one person
- Costs limited to a subset — "only the three drinkers split the bar tab"

It's the most precise method, but picking out everyone's amounts takes work. With a long receipt, don't split every line item perfectly — "manual for the big-ticket items, equal for the rest" is the sane compromise.

## 🧳 In practice, one trip mixes all three

You choose a method per expense, not per event. A three-day group trip might look like:

| Expense | Method | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Lodging | Equal | Everyone stays on equal terms |
| Rental car & gas | Equal (riders only) | Equal split among a subset |
| À la carte dinner | Manual | Orders varied wildly |
| Admission with a family's kid | Ratio | Adult and child prices differ |

Doing "a different method per expense" by hand falls apart fast, but in a bill-splitting app it's just a per-expense toggle. In Evere you pick Equal, Ratio, or Manual as the [split mode](/en/docs/expenses/) for each expense — and the final settlement combines everyone's balances across all of them regardless of method.

## 🧭 A decision flow for when you're unsure

1. Is each person's consumption clearly separate? → **Manual**
2. Is there a gap in ability to pay or usage that everyone acknowledges? → **Ratio**
3. Neither → **Equal**

The real point of this flow: when in doubt, equal is fine. Over-engineering fairness into complex math costs more than it saves — simple and accepted by everyone is what keeps group money flowing smoothly.

## ❓ FAQ

**Q. How do we pick the ratio numbers?**
Favor "memorable and round" over "precisely computed." Even if the income ratio is exactly 37:63, rounding to 40:60 is what keeps the system running.

**Q. Can we switch methods midstream?**
Absolutely — just don't retroactively reapply the new method to past expenses. Draw a line: "starting this month," "starting this trip." That's the trouble-free way to switch.

**Q. What about tiered splits — seniors pay more, juniors pay less?**
That's a form of ratio splitting. But for a group dinner, it's often faster to skip computing proportions and directly set stepped amounts — $60 / $50 / $40 — which is manual. Use whichever is quicker to agree on.
