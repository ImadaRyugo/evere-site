International couples have two more variables than couples from the same country: **currency, and the cost of living**. On top of the income conversation, "whose country's normal are we using?" creeps in — so even the word "half" stops meaning one thing.

This article sticks to those two. For the income gap itself, read [splitting expenses by income ratio](/en/blog/income-based-split-couples/) first.

## 🪙 Anchor on the country you live in

First decision: which currency you record and settle in. The rule is simple — **the currency of the country you're currently living in together**. That's where the groceries and the rent happen.

Long distance? Pick the currency where more of your shared spending happens. The country you meet in more often, or the one you travel in.

If it's genuinely a toss-up, just pick. What matters isn't choosing fairly; it's fixing on one. Run two currencies in parallel and every rate move quietly changes what your balance means.

## 💱 Build a setup where the rate stops mattering

Even with an anchor currency, payments in the other one are inevitable. When you visit them. When they front something at home.

Looking up a rate every time won't last. Record in the currency you paid and let the conversion happen automatically — that's the only version that survives contact with real life. The rate locks at the moment of recording, so a market move never rewrites your history ([which exchange rate should you use](/en/blog/exchange-rate-for-splitting/)).

International couples cross that border constantly, which is exactly why automatic conversion pays off more for you than for a tourist.

## ⚖️ Let the income ratio absorb the price gap

Here's the hard part. Two people in countries with different price levels, each putting in the same amount — is that fair?

The answer is: don't try to adjust for prices directly. Start down the road of "your country is at 70% of ours, so you pay 70%" and you'll argue forever about the basis — rent? groceries? overall? And a country's average price level has little to do with the two of you specifically.

Instead, split by take-home income ratio, and the price gap gets absorbed for free. Someone living where things are cheaper typically earns at local rates too. Split by income and the weight lands the same on each of you, in each of your lives.

> One person takes home the equivalent of $2,000, the other $3,200. Shared expenses split 40/60.

One line does more work — and lasts longer — than any cost-of-living index.

## ✈️ Who pays for the visits

The cost specific to long-distance international couples is the flights. One of you gets on a plane every time, so left alone it becomes a structure where one person just keeps paying.

Three approaches actually work.

| Approach | How it works | Fits when |
|---|---|---|
| Take turns | Whoever travels buys their own ticket | Distance and airfare are comparable |
| Flights are a shared expense | The ticket gets split like anything else | Only one of you ever travels |
| Traveler pays airfare, host covers the stay | Split travel from on-the-ground costs | One country is much pricier |

None of them is the right answer. But if "whoever visits pays for everything" has just been happening without anyone deciding it, talk about it once. Airfare is the single biggest shared cost you have; let it skew and no amount of tuning elsewhere catches up.

## 📝 Records travel across a language gap

One practical note to close on. International couples have money conversations in a language that isn't native to at least one of you. Nuance gets lost, and "did that come out colder than I meant?" accumulates.

Numbers and records are a real ally here. Amounts and dates need no translation. Shrink what has to be discussed; grow what can simply be checked. That's where a shared record earns its keep for an international couple.

## ❓ FAQ

**Q. I don't know what my partner earns in their currency.**
You don't need the exact figure. "Roughly 1.5× mine" is enough to land on 40/60. What matters is both of you acknowledging that a gap exists.

**Q. What if we marry and live in the same country?**
The anchor currency becomes the only currency, so it's just a normal couple's split. The price-level question disappears too.

**Q. Who eats the transfer fees?**
International transfers are expensive, so cutting the number of settlements does the most work. Move from settling every time to once a month and the fees drop to a twelfth. Split them or let the sender carry them — either is fine, as long as you decide first.
