The single most important principle for group trip money: **stop letting the organizer front everything, and switch to "whoever can pay, pays — and everything gets recorded."** Concentrating the fronting on one person maximizes their burden, the complexity of settling, and the risk of never getting paid back.

This article breaks down what to do across three phases: before, during, and after.

## Before you go: only three things to decide

### 1. Agree on the budget

Line up expectations on "roughly what this trip costs per person." Hotel tier, how often you eat out, whether you rent a car — these swing the per-person total enormously. Leave this misaligned and you'll hear "I wasn't planning to spend that much" mid-trip.

### 2. Split up the big payments

Lodging, transport, the rental car — the prepaid items are large, so spread them across people rather than piling them on one. "A books the hotel, B the rental car, C the outbound tickets" evens out how much each person is fronting, which also shrinks the transfers at settlement.

### 3. Decide how you'll record

"Someone will remember" is a guaranteed failure. Standardize the method as a group before you leave. Create a group in a bill-splitting app (in Evere, a [project](/en/docs/projects/)) and invite everyone, and on the day it's just one line from whoever paid. Going abroad? Check that it can record in multiple currencies.

## During the trip: just hold the line on recording in the moment

### Ten seconds, on the spot

There's exactly one rule during the trip: when money is spent, record it right then. "I'll do it later" doesn't survive till evening. Amount, who paid, what it was — plus the currency if you're abroad — is enough.

### Expenses where not everyone participates

Record group-wide costs (lodging, transport) separately from partial ones (optional tours, a second round of drinks, souvenirs). If your app lets you pick who an expense is for, just check the participants. Sloppily dividing everything by headcount is how resentment builds in the people who didn't go.

### Don't worry about cash vs. card

There's no need to fret that "money paid by card is somehow worth something different." Keep the record at face value and treat any points or fees from the payment method as that person's own. It's the simplest rule that holds up.

## After you're home: settle within 24 hours

### Consolidate into one payment per person

Rather than paying back individual IOUs, take each person's paid-minus-owed difference (the net) and get the transfers down to at most the number of people minus one. If the records are all there, your app's [settlement](/en/docs/settlement/) feature does this automatically.

### "Numbers locked on the ride home, transferred by tomorrow"

Settling is a race against time. While memories are fresh and everyone's still in it — concretely, lock the amounts on the way home and finish the transfers within 24 hours. Ninety percent of "let's settle once things calm down" quietly dies, and the person who fronted the money either eats the loss or sends an awkward reminder months later.

### Draw a line once you've settled

When it's done, mark it in the record: everything up to here is settled. When "wait, was that taxi included?" surfaces later, having a settled record is the difference between a ten-second answer and an argument.

## Three agreements that prevent trouble

Three things worth confirming aloud before departure:

1. **Don't sweat the small change** — round the final amounts. Ease beats precision
2. **Let conversion happen automatically** (if abroad) — don't debate exchange rates. See [how to split bills when currencies don't match](/en/blog/split-bills-different-currencies/)
3. **If it's not in the record, it's not in the settlement** — start trading "things I forgot to record" after the fact and it never ends. This is the trade-off that comes with recording diligently

## ❓ FAQ

**Q. What if someone runs out of money mid-trip?**
Someone else fronts it and records it like any other expense. "Money lent" and "a meal fronted" are the same thing as far as the settlement math is concerned.

**Q. Isn't this a big burden on the organizer / record-keeper?**
Make the rule "whoever paid records it themselves" and the role of record-keeper disappears entirely. When everyone can see the app's records, verification happens naturally too.

**Q. I don't want to be seen as the person who nitpicks about money.**
"I'll pop it in the app" is actually a far lighter thing to say than "I'll be billing you for this later." Letting a system hold the records is how you separate money from the friendship — which is the whole point of recording.
