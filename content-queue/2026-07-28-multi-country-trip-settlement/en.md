The first decision on a multi-country trip is whether to **settle per currency or combine everything**. On a trip with euros, baht, and dollars, getting this wrong buries you in transfers.

The answer is to combine. Not because it's fairer, but because of how many transfers you end up making.

## Splitting by currency kills the offsetting

Why combine? Plenty of people feel that settling per currency sounds more accurate, so let's look at an actual example.

Three friends travel through Paris, Bangkok, and New York, and one person fronts the lodging in each city.

- Paris: A fronts €300 (for all three)
- Bangkok: B fronts ฿9,000 (for all three)
- New York: C fronts $600 (for all three)

Settle per currency and you get this:

| Currency | Transfers |
|---|---|
| Euro | B pays A €100; C pays A €100 |
| Baht | A pays B ฿3,000; C pays B ฿3,000 |
| Dollar | A pays C $200; B pays C $200 |

**Six transfers, across three currencies.** Look at A: paying B while also receiving from B. That round trip is exactly what stops offsetting the moment you split by currency.

## Combine, and it's two transfers

Now the same trip converted into one currency. Say €1 = $1.10 and ฿1 = $0.03 (illustrative numbers).

| | Paid | Owes | Net |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | $330 | $400 | −$70 (pays) |
| B | $270 | $400 | −$130 (pays) |
| C | $600 | $400 | +$200 (receives) |

$1,200 across three people is $400 each. Net it out and **A pays C $70, B pays C $130**. Done.

Six transfers in three currencies became two in one. Transfer fees and exchange hassle collapse into a single line. The net-amount math behind this is in [how to settle up in the fewest payments](/en/blog/minimize-settlements/).

## Why splitting by country feels right

The urge to split per currency usually shows up when **the group changes between countries**. Someone joined late in Bangkok; only two of you did Paris.

But that's not a currency problem — it's a participants problem. What needs splitting isn't the currency; it's who each expense is divided among. If you can pick participants when recording, it resolves without touching the currency.

Put differently: currency is just the unit you standardize once at the end. It has nothing to do with the settlement logic.

## Keep the conversion timing consistent

The one real question with combining is when you take the rate. Multi-country trips run long, so the rate on day one differs from the rate on the last day.

Lock the rate at the moment each expense is recorded and the problem never arises. ## ❓ FAQ

**Q. One person did their own thing in one country.**
Split that country's expenses among the people who were there. It has nothing to do with whether you combine currencies.

**Q. What about leftover foreign cash at the end?**
Keep it out of the settlement. Leftover cash belongs to whoever exchanged it, and dragging it in makes the math unmanageable.

**Q. Does this really work with more than three currencies?**
The number of currencies is irrelevant. Convert everything into one, total it, and compute net amounts. Ten currencies, same steps.
