Your expense records fall apart abroad not because you forget amounts, but because **each payment method breaks your records for a different reason**. Cash, cards, and mobile payments each have their own trap.

The fix is the same for all three: record it on the spot, at face value. Here's why every other approach stops working.

## 💴 Cash: often there's no receipt

Street food, taxis, tips — cash payments frequently leave no paper trail. And because the amounts are small, they pile up, so by evening you're asking "that 300 baht, what was that for again?"

With cash, the moment it leaves your wallet is the only moment you can record it. No bank statement will reconstruct the breakdown later. Unlike a card, if you don't write it down, it really is gone.

## 💳 Cards: the statement shows up days later

The awkward thing about paying by card is that you can't know the exact charge at the time. It lands on your statement days later, with a foreign transaction fee baked in.

This is where people decide "I don't need to record it, I'll check my statement later." That fails twice over. The statement arrives after you're home, too late for settling. And the amount on it includes the fee, so it never matches the number you saw on the receipt.

### Watch out for "would you like to pay in your home currency?"

Use a card abroad and the terminal may ask whether you want to pay in the local currency or your own. That's DCC (Dynamic Currency Conversion), and choosing your home currency means the rate comes from the merchant or their payment processor rather than your card network. It's generally considered the worse deal.

For splitting purposes, DCC also complicates your records. Choose the local currency and the receipt's face amount is simply the number you record.

## 📱 Mobile and QR payments: the history is in mixed currencies

App-based payments feel safe because they keep a history, but the display currency varies by app. Some show the local currency; others show an already-converted amount in your home currency. Use a converted figure and it no longer shares a basis with everything else you recorded in the local currency.

Even when the history exists, the number you record is the one on the receipt.

## 📏 Standardize on face amounts

The shared fix across all three is to record face amounts.

| Payment method | Record this | Not this |
|---|---|---|
| Cash | The local-currency amount on the receipt (or what you handed over) | Your home currency, divided by the rate you exchanged at |
| Card | The local-currency amount on the receipt | The charge on your card statement |
| Mobile / QR | The local-currency amount on the receipt | Whatever the app converted it to |

In other words: whatever you paid with, record the number you saw at the till. Fees and points belong to the person who chose the payment method. The full reasoning is in [how to split bills abroad](/en/blog/split-bills-different-currencies/).

## 🔁 Don't reconcile against card statements

After settling, someone checks their statement and finds the charge doesn't match the split. It's tempting to redo the settlement to match. Don't.

The reason is simple: once everyone starts doing it, it never ends. Fee rates differ from card to card, and the cash payers have no statement at all. Reconciling everyone's statements isn't worth holding up the settlement for. Drawing the line at face amounts isn't giving up on accuracy — it's the only basis all of you can actually stand on.

How to handle the rate itself is compared in [which exchange rate should you use](/en/blog/exchange-rate-for-splitting/).

## ❓ FAQ

**Q. I forgot to get a receipt.**
Record it from memory. Having a record matters more than precision to the cent. A missing entry is a far bigger problem than being a little off.

**Q. Should card points be factored into the split?**
Usually not. Points belong to whoever chose the payment method. If it bothers the group, rotate who fronts the bill and it evens out on its own.

**Q. What about something paid on a family member's card?**
Record the payer as whoever actually paid at the till. Whose name is on the card is a separate question, and mixing the two only muddies things.
